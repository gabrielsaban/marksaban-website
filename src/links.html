Tautegorical Imperative

The term 'tautegorical' is used to describe a symbol that means what it is, as opposed to an 'allegorical' symbol, which points towards something beyond itself. In the article, I explore this concept in relation to Jung's psychological approach and how it affects our understanding of psychic phenomena.

I propose that Jung's psychology follows what might be called a 'tautegorical imperative' - that we need to approach psychological material on its own terms rather than reducing it to interpretations or causes. This perspective is especially important when working with dreams, symptoms, and archetypal material in therapy.

The paper considers how this approach differs from more reductive psychologies and examines the implications for contemporary analytical practice.

Theatre & Psyche

Drawing on my background in theatre and subsequent work as a psychotherapist, this paper explores the deep connections between theatrical performance and psychological processes. It examines how theatrical space creates a container for psychological transformation similar to the analytic setting.

The work considers how the dramatic enactment of psychological material provides a form of active imagination that can engage both performers and audience in meaningful psychological work. It discusses how theatrical metaphors can help us understand the therapeutic process, and conversely, how psychological insights can illuminate theatrical practice.

Through examining specific theatrical traditions and techniques, the paper suggests ways in which the two fields might continue to inform and enrich each other.

Dreamwork

This article explores the Jungian approach to working with dreams in analytical psychology. Unlike Freudian analysis, which often treats dreams as disguised wish fulfillments, Jungian dreamwork treats the dream as a direct communication from the unconscious that requires no translation.

The paper outlines practical methods for engaging with dream material, including amplification, active imagination, and the importance of considering both personal and archetypal dimensions. It explores how dreams function as compensatory messages from the psyche, offering perspectives that consciousness has overlooked.

Special attention is given to recurring dreams, nightmares, and the appearance of archetypal figures in dreams, with suggestions for how these might be approached in therapeutic work.
